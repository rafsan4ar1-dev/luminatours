Stargazing at Aoraki / Mount Cook: A Night in New Zealand's Dark Sky Reserve

A night beneath one of the darkest skies on Earth, and what it takes to photograph it.

There are dark skies, and then there is Aoraki / Mount Cook. Stargazing here means standing beneath the Aoraki Mackenzie International Dark Sky Reserve, one of the largest and darkest protected night skies on Earth, where the Milky Way can be bright enough to cast a faint shadow and the silence is total.

Inside the Aoraki Mackenzie International Dark Sky Reserve

Spanning 4,367 square kilometres across Aoraki / Mount Cook National Park and the Mackenzie Basin, the reserve was created in 2012, the first International Dark Sky Reserve in the Southern Hemisphere and the first anywhere to be awarded gold tier status, the highest there is. On the Bortle scale, which measures how dark a night sky really is, it sits at level two, meaning almost no light pollution and a sky close to what our ancestors saw. For stargazing and astrophotography, there are very few places on the planet to match it.

What you can see in the southern night sky

From down here in the far south, the sky looks unfamiliar to many first-time visitors, and all the more magical for it. On a clear, moonless night at Mount Cook you can see the bright core of the Milky Way arching overhead, the Southern Cross with its two Pointer stars, and the Large and Small Magellanic Clouds, two dwarf galaxies visible to the naked eye. Add planets, star clusters, the occasional satellite and a shooting star or two, and it becomes the kind of sky that makes people fall quiet.

The best time of year for stargazing at Mount Cook

You can stargaze here year round, but the clearest, crispest skies tend to arrive in winter, from roughly May to September, when the cold, dry air holds less moisture. The brilliant galactic core of the Milky Way rides highest from autumn through spring. Whatever the season, the single most important factor is the moon: a new moon, or a night after the moon has set, gives the darkest sky and the most stars. We plan every night around it.

Photographing the night sky

What your eyes see is breathtaking. What a camera can gather is something else again. With long exposures, a camera collects light your eyes never could, drawing colour and depth out of the Milky Way and placing you, small and still, beneath all of it. On a private night out I handle the technical side, the settings, the focus, the framing, while you simply take it in. A few days later, a gallery of edited astrophotography lands in your inbox: the night you can hardly believe you saw, made into something you can keep.

The Milky Way here can be bright enough to cast a shadow, and after all these years, it still stops me.

Frequently asked questions

Can you see the Milky Way at Aoraki / Mount Cook?

Yes. On a clear, moonless night the Milky Way is vivid to the naked eye, including its bright galactic core. Aoraki / Mount Cook sits inside the Aoraki Mackenzie International Dark Sky Reserve, one of the darkest skies on Earth, where the Milky Way is often bright enough to cast a faint shadow.

When is the best time of year for stargazing in New Zealand?

Stargazing is possible year round, but winter, roughly May to September, brings the clearest, driest skies in the South Island. For the brightest Milky Way, plan a trip around a new moon between autumn and spring.

Where is the best stargazing near Mount Cook?

The entire Aoraki Mackenzie International Dark Sky Reserve offers world-class skies, including Aoraki / Mount Cook National Park and the shores of Lake Pukaki and Lake Tekapo. A private tour lets you reach quiet, dark spots well away from any lights.

Do I need my own camera for astrophotography?

No. On a photographer-led tour your host captures the night for you with professional astrophotography equipment and delivers edited images afterwards. You are welcome to bring your own camera if you would like guidance.
